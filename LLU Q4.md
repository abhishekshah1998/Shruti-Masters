4. Loma Linda University believes deeply in integrating spiritual values into the educational experience. As a result, religion courses and chapel attendance are part of the curriculum. Tell us why you believe such a faith-based education would be of special benefit to you. (4000 characters max)

**DRAFT ANSWER:**
I grew up in a Jain household. In Jainism, ethics is not a subject you study and set aside — it is the architecture of daily life. Ahimsa, non-harming, extends not just to action but to intention. Aparigraha, non-possessiveness, asks you to ask constantly: am I taking more than I need? These were not abstract principles for me. They were the lens through which I understood what it meant to be in a room with someone who is suffering.

When I began my clinical rotations, that lens revealed itself in ways I did not expect. In the CVTS ward at D.Y. Patil Hospital, I worked with patients just 24–48 hours post-cardiac surgery. One man could not hold a pencil. Another was relearning how to breathe. They were not asking me for technique — they were asking me to witness their vulnerability with complete presence. At sixteen, I had been that patient, a girl with an ATF ligament tear who had to rebuild her body through sheer patience and trust in her physiotherapist. I knew what it felt like when a clinician carried you not just with their hands, but with their attention to who you were.

Still, something was missing. My BPT's Bioengineering and Professional Ethics module — taken in Semester 6 — covered four areas I found genuinely important: privacy and confidentiality, equality and justice and equity, non-discrimination and non-stigmatization, and respect for cultural diversity and pluralism. These are serious principles. But the syllabus itself was candid about its limits: it stated explicitly that the bioethics content "does not have a complete course" and should be treated as "a source of inspiration," not a comprehensive curriculum. Within the confidentiality unit, we studied justified breaches — mandatory reporting, serious danger to others, patient consent — but none of it was resolved in the way a profession demands. Within the equity unit, we mapped local, national, and global disparities in health access; we knew the map. No one taught us how to practice on the harder terrain of a system that routinely fails the patients we treat. The module named these tensions and moved on. The questions did not move with it.

A faith-based education at LLU would give those questions a home — and specific courses will close the gap. PTGR 553, Clinical Reasoning and Critical Thinking, directly trains clinicians to defend, justify, and rationalize clinical decisions: exactly the disposition I needed but was not given. PTGR 578, Medical Screening for Physical Therapists, builds the judgment to know when physiotherapy's scope ends and a medical referral begins — a boundary with both ethical and clinical weight. PTGR 594, Evidence-Based Practice, will help me connect what the research says to how I actually treat the human being in front of me. These are not just technical upgrades. At an institution where whole-person care is a stated standard, these courses are taught inside a value system, not alongside one.

I approach chapel with genuine curiosity, not obligation. Reading about LLU's understanding of the Sabbath — a weekly rhythm of rest, renewal, and reconnection with purpose — I was struck by how closely it parallels the Jain practice of *pratikramana*, a structured daily pause to reflect on one's actions and return to one's values. In high-volume clinical environments, it is easy to become efficient and forget to be present. Chapel would be an institutional counterweight to that drift: a regular, structured reminder that the work we do is not production, it is ministry — to borrow LLU's own language.

The Good Samaritan parable, central to LLU's community engagement philosophy, is one I read and recognized immediately. In Jainism, you serve because the other person is — regardless of their standing. That is the same moral logic Jesus articulated. The theological language is different; the obligation is identical. The Cornerstone Clinic in San Bernardino, where LLU students deliver free PT services to low-income residents, is where my BPT's unit on local health disparities becomes lived practice. My syllabus could describe inequity. LLU's curriculum asks you to act against it. That distinction is exactly why I am here.

I will graduate as a physical therapist who has been trained — not merely advised — to reflect on the moral weight of every clinical decision. A conventional DPT program will make me technically proficient. LLU's model will hold me to a higher integration: rigorous about evidence and rigorous about ethics simultaneously. That standard is not a burden. It is exactly what I came here to be trained by.
