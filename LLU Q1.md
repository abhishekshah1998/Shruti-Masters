1. Tell us what characteristics of Loma Linda University are particularly attractive and meaningful to you and why you have chosen to apply for advanced education. (4000 characters max) 

**DRAFT ANSWER:**
I first understood what it means to make a person whole not in a classroom, but on a basketball court. During my first year of physiotherapy school, I tore my Anterior Talofibular ligament and chose not to pursue surgery. A physiotherapist guided my entire recovery through targeted work and patience alone — no procedures, just my own strength and consistency. Watching my function and confidence rebuild gave me something no textbook could: a felt sense of what patients live through, and an unshakeable conviction that recovery is never only biomechanical. Fear, dignity, and support matter just as much as tissue biology.

That conviction is why Loma Linda University's motto — *To Make Man Whole* — is not just attractive to me. It is personal.

LLU's whole-person care framework — Physical, Relational, Intellectual, Spiritual, and Mental (PRISM) — gives language and structure to what I witnessed across 1,092 internship hours at Dr. D. Y. Patil Hospital. Post-cardiac surgery patients relearning how to breathe; a 16-year-old stroke survivor who needed trust before facilitation; community members at PCMC Divyang Bhavan Foundation and Amrutvel Old Age Home whose recovery was inseparable from their dignity. At each of those bedsides, excellent physical care was necessary but not sufficient. LLU is the only institution I have encountered that builds this understanding into its institutional DNA.

The second characteristic that draws me to LLU is its identity as a servant institution. The Parable of the Good Samaritan does not ask whether the person in need shares your background or your faith — it simply asks: will you stop? Some of my most formative experiences have been that kind of stopping — volunteering as on-field physiotherapist at the Apla Pune Marathon, the Indian Army Soldierathon, and the Bombay Sappers Soldierathon; managing injuries at a Maharashtra State Football Association youth tournament; teaching English to underprivileged children at a local orphanage. I want to train where service is a professional expectation, not an extracurricular option — and LLU, through its Street Medicine and Cornerstone Clinic outreach and the Global Health Institute's SIMS program and Malamulo Global Campus, makes that expectation structural.

Third, LLU's Postprofessional DPT curriculum provides the exact academic progression I seek. My BPT modules—specifically *Orthopaedics & Traumatology*, *Physiotherapeutic Skills*, and *Physical & Functional Diagnostic Skills*—and my internship at Dr. D. Y. Patil Hospital built strong foundations. Yet clinical practice revealed the gap between foundational competence and autonomous judgment. PTGR 553 (*Clinical Reasoning*) and PTGR 578 (*Medical Screening*) will bridge this gap, elevating my diagnostic skills to recognize non-neuromusculoskeletal red flags and justify decisions without protocol dependence. PTGR 579 (*Clinical Imaging*) advances my musculoskeletal assessment toolkit with active use of MRI and ultrasound. Finally, PTGR 514 (*Professional Systems*) and RELR 525 (*Christian Leadership*) formalize the administrative and servant-leadership values I exercised during four years on the student council.

Finally, LLU's vision — transforming lives through education, healthcare, and research — speaks to where I want to go. My published case study on Foville Syndrome, my STS Fellowship work on spinal mobilization, and first-place presentations at national conferences have shown me that I want to integrate evidence generation with advanced clinical practice: design protocols, test them on patients, and publish what I find. LLU is where I can build those capacities while staying true to the reason I chose this profession — the belief that helping one person reclaim their function, independence, and confidence is a day well lived.
