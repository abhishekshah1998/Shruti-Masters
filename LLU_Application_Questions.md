2. Loma Linda University's mission is to continue the teaching and healing ministry of Jesus Christ. The University realizes its mission through the values of compassion, excellence, humility, integrity, justice, teamwork and wholeness. Briefly explain how your personal values align with the values of Loma Linda University and how such values will guide your service to humanity. (4000 characters max)Required  

**DRAFT ANSWER:**
I was raised in a Jain household in India. Jainism is built on *Ahimsa* (non-violence and compassion toward all living beings), *Satya* (honesty), *Aparigraha* (non-possessiveness), and *Anekantavada* — the belief that truth is many-sided and that no one person holds the complete picture. Growing up, these were not abstract doctrines but lived values that shaped how my family treated others, how we approached service, and how we understood our responsibility to those around us.

When I encountered LLU's values — Compassion, Wholeness, Integrity, Humility, Justice, Teamwork, and Excellence — I felt an immediate and genuine recognition. Ahimsa *is* compassion. Anekantavada *is* humility — the willingness to acknowledge that my clinical perspective alone is incomplete, and that the patient's lived experience must be centered. The Jain commitment to non-possessiveness aligns with LLU's call to put self-importance aside for the greater good. These are not translations I had to force; the resonance was natural.

What LLU's values add to mine — and what I find distinctly compelling — is the framing of healthcare as a *calling*, not merely a profession. The *C WITH Jesus' Eyes* framework treats every clinical interaction as an opportunity to embody compassion and justice. That framing elevates my own understanding of what it means to serve. I don't enter a treatment room simply to reduce pain or restore mobility — I enter to restore dignity, function, and hope to a full human being. LLU's motto, *To Make Man Whole*, captures exactly the kind of clinician I am determined to become.

These values will guide my service to humanity in concrete ways. My Jain upbringing instilled in me a commitment to serve those most in need — which is why, in my third year of BPT, I voluntarily taught English to underprivileged children at a local orphanage on weekends, outside of any curriculum requirement. During my clinical internship, I delivered community-based physiotherapy at PCMC Divyang Bhavan Foundation (serving differently abled individuals), Amrutvel Old Age Home, and a rural health training centre in Alandi — placements I embraced not as obligations, but as the most meaningful parts of my training. I have also served as an on-field physiotherapist at military endurance events — the Indian Army Soldierathon and Bombay Sappers Soldierathon — experiences that reinforced for me that service belongs wherever people are vulnerable, not only within hospital walls. I am drawn to LLU's global mission work in underserved communities across Haiti, Ethiopia, Malawi, and beyond for exactly the same reason. I intend to participate in those opportunities and to carry this ethos of justice-driven, whole-person service into every community I work in throughout my career.

3. Loma Linda University believes deeply in integrating spiritual values into the educational experience. As a result, religion courses and chapel attendance are part of the curriculum. Tell us why you believe such a faith-based education would be of special benefit to you. (4000 characters max)Required  

**DRAFT ANSWER:**
A faith-based education at LLU would offer me something that no purely clinical curriculum can: the structured space to examine *why* I am in healthcare, not just *how* to practice it well. That distinction matters enormously to me.

I come from a Jain background, where ethics is not a subject studied and set aside — it is a way of living. In my BPT program, the module I found most transformative was Bioengineering and Professional Ethics, precisely because it forced me to sit with difficult questions: What do I owe a patient whose choices I disagree with? Where does clinical judgment end and moral responsibility begin? How do I serve someone whose suffering I cannot fully fix? LLU's religion and ethics courses promise to take those questions further, grounding them in a rich moral and theological tradition. That is not a burden — it is an intellectual and professional gift.

Chapel attendance is something I approach with genuine curiosity rather than obligation. Reading about LLU's understanding of the Sabbath — as a weekly practice of rest, renewal, and reconnection with purpose — I was struck by how closely it echoes something in my own tradition. Jainism also values periods of stillness and withdrawal from the noise of ambition, as a way of remembering what truly matters. For me, chapel would serve as a structured anchor: a regular reminder that the work I do is in service of something larger than productivity or career advancement.

The specific benefit to me, practically and professionally, is this: I will graduate not only as a skilled clinician but as one who has been trained to reflect on the moral weight of every clinical decision. LLU's model of whole-person care — treating body, mind, and spirit — is not a philosophy I simply agree with intellectually; it is the kind of practitioner I am actively trying to become. A faith-based environment will hold me to that standard in a way that a conventional program would not. I expect it to make me more empathetic, more ethically rigorous, and more equipped to serve diverse patients with the dignity and wholeness that LLU's mission demands.

4. Tell us what characteristics of Loma Linda University are particularly attractive and meaningful to you and why you have chosen to apply for advanced education. (4000 characters max) 

**DRAFT ANSWER:**
What is most attractive to me about Loma Linda University is that it treats advanced physical therapy education as more than technical training. LLU brings together rigorous clinical science, ethical formation, service, and whole-person healing. Its motto, *To Make Man Whole*, is especially meaningful to me because it reflects the kind of physiotherapist I want to become: one who does not see a diagnosis in isolation, but understands a patient's physical function, emotional resilience, environment, and long-term quality of life as interconnected.

Several characteristics of LLU stand out to me. First is its explicit commitment to wholeness. During my Bachelor of Physiotherapy and my 1,092-hour rotatory internship, I worked with orthopaedic, neurological, cardiopulmonary, community-based, and ICU patients. Those experiences taught me that recovery is rarely only biomechanical. Fear, deconditioning, confidence, family support, motivation, and dignity all influence clinical outcomes. LLU's whole-person philosophy gives structure and purpose to what I have already begun to see in practice.

Second, I am deeply drawn to LLU's service-oriented identity. The Good Samaritan model of community engagement, the Street Medicine and Cornerstone Clinic outreach, and the Global Health Institute opportunities such as SIMS and the Malamulo Global Campus show that service is not peripheral at LLU; it is part of the institution's core. That is meaningful to me because some of the most formative parts of my own training have been community-based physiotherapy at PCMC Divyang Bhavan Foundation, Amrutvel Old Age Home, and the rural health training centre in Alandi, along with volunteering as an on-field physiotherapist at large sporting events and teaching underprivileged children at an orphanage. I want to study in a place where service to underserved communities is treated as a professional responsibility, not an optional activity.

Third, LLU's Postprofessional DPT curriculum is exactly the kind of advanced education I am seeking. My BPT gave me strong foundations in orthopaedics, neurology, cardiopulmonary rehabilitation, biomechanics, manual therapy, research methodology, and professional ethics. My internship at Dr. D. Y. Patil Hospital and my clinical experience at Capri Spine Clinic strengthened those foundations further. At the same time, these experiences showed me the limits of undergraduate preparation. I now want the next level of education that will help me think and practice more independently. Courses such as PTGR 553 Clinical Reasoning and Critical Thinking in Physical Therapy, PTGR 578 Medical Screening for Physical Therapists, PTGR 579 Clinical Imaging for Physical Therapist, PTGR 514 Professional Systems in Management I, and RELR 525 Health Care and the Dynamics of Christian Leadership are especially attractive to me because they develop the exact capacities I want to build: stronger diagnostic judgment, recognition of red flags, evidence-based decision-making, and leadership grounded in service.

I have chosen to apply for advanced education because I do not want to remain at the level of competent foundational practice. I want to become an autonomous, evidence-based, globally minded physical therapist who can evaluate complex patients more deeply, justify clinical decisions more rigorously, and contribute to the profession through leadership and research. LLU is meaningful to me because it offers not only advanced coursework, but also a philosophy of healthcare that unites excellence with compassion and knowledge with service. That combination is what makes LLU the right place for my next stage of growth.
