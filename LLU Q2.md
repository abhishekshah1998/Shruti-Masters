2. Loma Linda University's mission is to continue the teaching and healing ministry of Jesus Christ. The University realizes its mission through the values of compassion, excellence, humility, integrity, justice, teamwork and wholeness. Briefly explain how your personal values align with the values of Loma Linda University and how such values will guide your service to humanity. (4000 characters max)Required  

**ANSWER:**

I was raised in a Jain household in India, in a tradition built on *Ahimsa* (non-violence and compassion), *Satya* (truth and honesty), *Aparigraha* (non-possessiveness and humility), and *Anekantavada* — the conviction that truth is many-sided, and no single perspective holds the complete picture. These were the grammar of daily life — shaping how my family cared for neighbors and approached disagreement. Long before I entered a clinic, these values were forming the physiotherapist I intended to become.

When I first encountered LLU's values, what struck me was their depth. *Ahimsa* is an insistence that every living being deserves protection from suffering — the same conviction animating LLU's vision of treating every patient as a whole person, made in the image of God. *Anekantavada* is Humility made systematic: recognizing that my clinical assessment is merely one lens, and the patient's lived experience is essential to the truth of a case. *Aparigraha* maps directly onto LLU's call to put self-importance aside for the greater good, and onto the Teamwork it takes to collaborate.

LLU's PRISM framework adds the profound dimension of *Wholeness*. In my BPT, I learned to treat the body; in my internships, I learned healing requires more. Caring for post-op cardiovascular patients relearning to breathe, or a 16-year-old stroke survivor needing trust before physical facilitation, I realized treating only the body is incomplete. This commitment to wholeness is why I value curriculum like **PTGR 515 (Cardiopulmonary Assessment, Wellness)**; managing chronic disease requires addressing whole-person lifestyle and wellness, not just acute symptoms.

*Integrity* and *Satya* ask me to be honest when uncertain and trustworthy when a patient is vulnerable. When patient progress stalled during an orthopedic rotation, my response was not to default to a protocol or document false progress, but to re-evaluate and consult. That commitment to truth is precisely why I seek the rigor of **PTGR 553 (Clinical Reasoning)** and **PTGR 578 (Medical Screening)**. Integrity in practice means having the critical thinking to justify complex decisions, and the diagnostic honesty to recognize medical red flags and refer patients out when appropriate.

*Justice* is where Jainism and LLU's mission speak most clearly to each other. The Hebrew prophets described justice as care for the vulnerable—resistance to inequity. Jainism teaches that allowing others to suffer when you can help is itself a form of harm. This is why I volunteered to teach English at a local orphanage, why my community postings serving specially abled individuals and the elderly were so meaningful, and why I served as an on-field physiotherapist at endurance events like the Indian Army Soldierathon. Service goes where the need is. 

This is why LLU's global mission resonates so deeply. The work in Haiti, Ethiopia, Malawi, and beyond—bringing therapy to patients who may never otherwise receive it—is the program's deepest expression. Coupled with the leadership skills from **PTGR 514 (Professional Systems in Management)**, I intend to participate in those opportunities, carrying this ethos of justice-driven, *C WITH Jesus' Eyes* service into every community I serve throughout my career.
